USE CASES

Use Case 1: Navigating and Obstacle Avoidance

 The hazard map, continuously updated in real-time, acts as the digital eyes of lunar landers. It identifies hazardous terrain features, ensuring that the lander avoids rough or unsafe areas during its descent and landing phases. Additionally, during surface navigation, the map assists in identifying obstacles, such as large rocks or craters, allowing the lander to adjust its route, avoiding potential collisions.

• Impacts: Precise navigation and obstacle avoidance are paramount for the safety of the mission. By avoiding hazardous terrain and obstacles, the lander ensures a stable landing, protecting both equipment and astronauts. This use case's impact reverberates throughout the mission, ensuring the success of subsequent exploration activities.











Use Case 2: Lunar Landers and Rovers

 For lunar landers, the hazard map is invaluable in selecting the landing site. It evaluates the terrain, ensuring a flat and stable surface, minimizing risks during landing. For rovers, the map guides their path, ensuring they explore scientifically valuable areas. The map aids in identifying unique geological formations, enabling targeted scientific investigations.

• Impacts: Efficient landing and strategic exploration maximize the scientific output of lunar missions. By landing in optimal locations and exploring specific sites of interest, scientists can gather diverse data, deepening our understanding of lunar geology, composition, and evolution.











Use Case 3: Planetary Science and Exploration

 Planetary scientists rely on the hazard map to study specific lunar features. It aids in selecting research sites, allowing scientists to explore areas rich in particular minerals or geological formations. Scientists can conduct in-depth analyses of these sites, unravelling the Moon's geological history.

• Impacts: Enhanced research opportunities lead to groundbreaking discoveries. Detailed studies of lunar features provide insights not only into the Moon's history but also into broader planetary processes. These discoveries contribute to our understanding of celestial bodies, planetary formation, and the conditions necessary for life.











Use Case 4: Robotic Missions

 Autonomous robotic missions utilize the hazard map to make real-time decisions. When faced with unexpected terrain challenges, the map guides the robots in finding alternate routes. The robots can also identify and study interesting surface features, such as rare minerals, without human intervention.

• Impacts: Increased mission autonomy allows robots to adapt and explore challenging terrains independently. This autonomy optimizes mission efficiency, as robots can continue gathering valuable data even in complex and hazardous lunar landscapes.











Use Case 5: Lunar Map Image Resolution

 Higher-resolution lunar maps provide a wealth of data. Scientists can zoom in on specific features, craters and boulders, at a microscopic level. This level of detail aids in identifying mineral compositions, erosion patterns, and even potential areas for future human habitation.

• Impacts: Enhanced image resolution facilitates precise geological analysis, enabling scientists to conduct in-depth studies. This level of granularity is essential for comprehensive scientific investigations, shedding light on the Moon's geological, volcanic, and tectonic history in unprecedented detail.
